Welcome! I'm a cognitive neuroscientist, cognitive psychologist, and linguist.

My research focuses on how the brain translates from thought to language, using tools like ECoG and syntactic priming. ECoG is the recording of electrical activity directly from the surface of the brain in awake human patients. It has unparalleled spatial and temporal resolution, allowing us to know precisely where and when things are happening in the brain.

Words in the Brain

The human brain has 10s of 1000s of words stored in it somewhere. This project uses state of the art machine learning and electrocorticography (ECoG, or measures of brain activity taken directly from the surface of the brain in awake neurosurgical patients) to predict what speakers are going to say up to 0.7 seconds before they begin speaking. We use this methodology to visualize the different stages of representation that words pass through (conceptual, phonemic, articulatory) as the brain accesses and prepares to say a word aloud.

Generalization in Artificial Language Learning

The world's 6000 languages have a surprising number of similarities in their grammatical structures. One common pattern is a contingency hierarchy: if some langauge has Structure X, then it necessarily has Structure Y. In this project, Vic Ferreira and I taught subjects fake languages with Structure X. Then, we ask them to describe pictures that require them to use Structure Y. Many prominent theories predict that humans can only learn structures we have been exposed to. Contrary to this prediction, participants spontaneously produced Property Y, even though they had never heard it before.

Selected publications

  • Morgan & Ferreira 2021: Beyond Input: Language learners produce novel relative clause types without exposure. Journal of Cognitive Psychology.

Resumptive Pronouns

In many languages, speakers produce pronouns that no one is sure whether they are grammatical (← case in point). These resumptive pronouns sound bad, but speakers still say them (see my 2018 paper with Matt Wagers). What does this mean for theories of syntax? Along with Aya Meltzer-Asscher, Julie Fadlon, Vic Ferreira, Titus von der Malsburg, Matt Wagers, and Eva Wittenberg, I am working on several projects which converge on the idea that the weird properties of resumptive pronouns result from cognitive limitations during speech.

Selected publications

  • Morgan & Wagers (2018). English resumptive pronouns are more common where gaps are less acceptable. Linguistic Inquiry.
  • Fadlon, Morgan, Meltzer-Asscher, & Ferreira (2019). It depends: Optionality in the production of filler-gap dependencies. Journal of Memory and Language.
  • Morgan, von der Malsburg, Ferreira, Wittenberg (2020). Shared syntax between comprehension and production: Multi-paradigm evidence that resumptive pronouns hinder comprehension. Cognition.

Individual Differences in Syntax

Does every native speaker of English have the same grammar? In this project I demonstrate that there are as many versions of English grammar as there are speakers of English. This finding suggests that the grammar is probably not a set of abstract "rules" derived from experience. Instead, these rules are similar to phonemes: emergent properties of a lifetime of experience.
